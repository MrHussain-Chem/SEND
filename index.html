How to use this page
Every strategy listed here has direct RCT or meta-analysis evidence and is applicable from day one of teaching. Crucially, all benefit students with SEND and raise outcomes for the whole class. This is not a bolt-on list — these are core teaching habits to build and make automatic. Strategies are rated using the EEF Teaching and Learning Toolkit where applicable. Only approaches with proven whole-class efficacy are included.

Teaching assistant guidance

The evidence problem with unstructured TA deployment

Research consistently shows that unstructured TA deployment — sitting beside students, copying notes, or completing tasks for them — leads to negative effects on pupil independence and attainment (Blatchford et al. DISS Project; EEF, 2025). The EEF's updated 2025 guidance (52 studies) is unequivocal: TAs supplement teaching; they do not replace it. A DfE 2024 survey found 23% of secondary TAs feel insufficiently trained for their role. This page provides the actionable tools drawn directly from that evidence base.

EEF Deployment of Teaching Assistants — 5 recommendations (2025)

Stop / Start: behaviours to eliminate and replace

The "Least Help First" escalation framework

TAs must always begin at the left and only move right when the previous level has not been sufficient. Jumping to the right undermines independence.

Brain → Board → Book → Buddy → Boss (EEF, 2025)
Before asking for help: Brain (what do I know?) → Board (is it displayed?) → Book (in my notes?) → Buddy (ask a peer) → Boss (ask teacher/TA). TAs coach students through this sequence rather than shortcutting to the answer.

Metacognitive questioning bank

Replace all closed questions ("Do you understand?", "Shall I help?") with these. Print and laminate for TA lanyards.

15-minute pre-lesson briefing protocol

The briefing should cover:
  • 📌 Lesson objective and learning steps
  • 📌 Which students are likely to struggle and why
  • 📌 Which scaffolds to use and when to deploy them
  • 📌 When the TA should step back and allow productive struggle
  • 📌 Which students the TA monitors vs. teacher monitors
  • 📌 Key vocabulary to pre-teach before the lesson starts
Structured interventions: where TAs have the strongest evidence
Meta-analysis (EEF, 2025): structured TA-led 1:1 or small-group interventions produce an average effect size of +0.35 (~4 months additional progress).
Required conditions: a structured evidence-based programme · explicit TA training · regular uninterrupted sessions · shared progress monitoring · teacher review · student does not miss core instruction to attend.

Scaffolding for independence — graduated release

Stage 1 — Model
TA thinks aloud completing a similar task. Student watches. No output expected yet.
Stage 2 — Guided practice
TA and student work together. TA uses metacognitive questions. Student contributes most of the thinking.
Stage 3 — Supported independence
Student works alone. TA observes from a distance and intervenes only after a 60-second productive struggle window.

Ofsted framework 2025 — what changed and what it means for SEND

The biggest inspection reform in a decade — in effect from 10 November 2025
Single-word grades (Outstanding / Good / Requires Improvement / Inadequate) have been abolished. Every school now receives a detailed report card graded across up to 11 evaluation areas. For the first time, Inclusion is a standalone evaluation area — it is no longer buried inside other judgements. Sir Martyn Oliver, HMCI, has stated he wants the framework to produce "a system-wide behaviour shift on inclusion".

The 5-point grading scale

Exceptional
Practice "among the very best nationally, which should be shared with other schools." Ofsted expects this to be very rarely awarded — it signals a national centre of excellence.
Strong standard
All expected and strong standards met. Excellent, consistent work making a real difference for pupils.
Expected standard
The school is doing everything it should be doing. A high bar — not a mediocre rating. Inspectors stress this means "doing the right things consistently and well".
Needs attention
Work required to reach expected standard. Not a fail — triggers monitoring inspections to track improvement.
Urgent improvement
Significant failures. Regional improvement teams deploy up to £100,000 support per school. Reserved for extreme cases.

Core evaluation areas

1. Achievement
What pupils know and can do; progress over time; outcomes for all groups including SEND and disadvantaged.
2. Curriculum and teaching
Curriculum design, sequencing, and accessibility. Whether teaching meets the needs of all learners. Scaffolding and adaptive practice.
3. Attendance and behaviour
Previously separate — now combined. Inspectors assess how schools foster positive relationships and routines to reduce absence and improve engagement.
4. Personal development and wellbeing
Character development, resilience, SMSC, health, relationships education. Staff wellbeing is now formally assessed here too.
5. Inclusion ★ STANDALONE — new
How well the school identifies and supports pupils facing barriers. Assessed for all pupils: SEND, disadvantaged, pupil premium, LAC/PLAC, EAL. Inclusion is no longer embedded in other judgements — it has its own grade.
6. Leadership and governance
Strategic vision, CPD, accountability, evidence-informed decision-making. Quality of SENDCo integration into leadership assessed here.
Safeguarding — Met / Not Met (separate)
Judged separately on a binary scale across six areas: leadership, staff training, record-keeping, safer recruitment, governance, and culture of vigilance.

What inspectors look for in Inclusion — the SEND-critical area

The inclusion evaluation area is directly aligned with SEND provision quality. Inspectors will want to see evidence of practice, not paperwork. The SENCO will play a central role in inspection conversations under this framework.

Identification and planning
  • Robust, timely SEND identification processes
  • Support plans that reflect individual needs — not generic
  • Evidence of the graduated approach (Assess–Plan–Do–Review)
  • EHC plans reviewed and updated meaningfully
In-classroom practice
  • Curriculum accessible for all — not parallel, separate work
  • Adaptive teaching embedded, not bolted on
  • Scaffolding used and withdrawn — not permanent crutches
  • TA deployment evidence-aligned (not proximity-based)
Outcomes and belonging
  • SEND pupils achieve, attend, and feel they belong
  • Attendance and exclusion data disaggregated by SEND type
  • Collaboration with parents/carers and external specialists
  • Pupil voice: SEND students can describe how they are supported
The "secure-fit" model — a significant change from "best-fit"
Under the new framework, inspectors use a secure-fit model: every criterion within a grade band must be met to achieve that grade. Under the old "best-fit" approach, schools could compensate for weaknesses with strengths elsewhere. This means a single weak area in Inclusion will hold a school at "expected standard" even if everything else is "strong". Schools must ensure SEND provision is genuinely strong across all criteria, not strong in some areas and weak in others.

Key implications for your practice

For teachers
  • ✦ Inspectors will observe how you adapt teaching in real time — adaptive teaching is no longer optional
  • ✦ Be ready to explain your approaches to SEND pupils to an inspector — not your policy, your actual practice
  • ✦ Inclusion is not a SENCO issue — every teacher's classroom will be evidence for the Inclusion grade
  • ✦ Curriculum accessibility is assessed directly: can all pupils, including those with SEND, access and make progress through your curriculum?
For the SENCO and leadership
  • ✦ The SENCO will speak directly with inspectors — inclusion strategy, graduated approach, and impact evidence must be clear and current
  • ✦ Pupil premium strategy must show evidence of impact, not just intent — inspectors will scrutinise outcomes
  • ✦ TA deployment must align with evidence — proximity-based support will be noted negatively
  • ✦ Attendance for SEND subgroups will be examined — data disaggregated by EHC/SEN support/no SEN is essential

UK schools context — what the national evidence shows

The honest picture: the attainment gap has not closed
Despite decades of SEND policy reform, the national attainment gap between students with and without SEND has remained large and has slightly widened. This context is important: improvement is possible, but there are no quick fixes, and no named programme has been shown by rigorous RCT to close this gap at scale. The strategies in this resource are drawn from the best available evidence on what works at the classroom level.

National attainment data

📊
50 percentage point gap (2023/24) — 72% of students with no identified SEN met expected standards vs. 22% with an EHC plan or SEN support. This gap was 48pp in 2015/16 and has not narrowed (Parliament Education Committee, 2024).
📉
Achievement for All — negative RCT result (EEF) — The most prominent whole-school SEND improvement programme produced 2 months' less progress in reading and maths for targeted children compared to control schools. This underscores why individual classroom strategies grounded in cognitive science matter more than branded programmes.
📋
Persistent absence disproportionately affects SEND pupils — 34% of pupils with EHC plans are persistently absent (2023/24) vs 14.7% of non-SEND pupils. Addressing attendance is inseparable from addressing attainment for this group.
⚠️
SEND identification is rising — 1.7 million pupils (17.5% of school population) are now identified with SEND in England. 93% of schools plan to increase TA numbers in response. Without training, this risks replicating the patterns the DISS project identified as harmful.

What school-level research shows does work

Rather than naming specific schools (attainment data for SEND subgroups at school level is rarely published consistently enough to verify claims), the strongest evidence comes from research on the characteristics of schools where all pupils, including those with SEND, make stronger progress.

School characteristics linked to stronger SEND outcomes
  • ✦ High-quality teaching as the primary lever — EEF consistently identifies classroom instruction quality as the strongest school-level factor for SEND outcomes, above any specialist programme or TA deployment model
  • ✦ Frequent peer classroom observation — Schools where teachers observe each other regularly show greater Progress 8 gains; leadership observation alone does not have the same effect (Taylor et al., 2024)
  • ✦ Evidence-informed decision-making at leadership level — School leaders of high-progress schools consistently cite use of research evidence as a driver of their schools' effectiveness
  • ✦ Shared, consistent language across the school — Strategies like Zones of Regulation and metacognitive questioning only produce strong effects when used consistently school-wide, not by individual teachers
  • ✦ SENCO with genuine influence on teaching & learning — Not just administrative. Effective SENCOs drive CPD, build TA capacity, and link strategy to classroom practice (EEF / nasen, 2025)
The London effect on SEND outcomes
Research consistently shows a "London effect" on Progress 8 scores. The London Borough of Barking and Dagenham (LBBD) is one documented example: SEND pupils' attainment on headline measures at EYFS and KS2 was above the England average in 2024/25, with the borough ranking 1st nationally for 3-year improvement across all pupils (LBBD Education Performance Report, 2025). This is attributed to sustained CPD investment, borough-wide consistency in strategy implementation, and a strong SEND inclusion framework — not any single programme.

Verified case study: Leigh Academy Halley, south-east London

Secondary 11–18 5 × Exceptional (Ofsted 2025) 40% FSM eligible Above-average EHC plans 30%+ EAL Kidbrooke, South-East London
Leigh Academy Halley was one of the first schools inspected under Ofsted's new November 2025 framework — as a volunteer school. It received "exceptional" in five areas: attendance and behaviour, inclusion, leadership and governance, personal development and wellbeing, and post-16 provision. Teaching and curriculum and achievement were graded "strong standard". Inspectors described it as "an exceptionally inclusive school" where "all pupils are welcome, and all are catered for extremely well" — producing "a transformational impact on the extent to which all pupils succeed personally and academically."
This is not a selective or low-need school. It serves one of the most deprived wards in the country, with above-average EHCP numbers, 40% FSM eligibility, 30%+ EAL students, and many pupils joining mid-year with gaps in learning. The outcome is therefore a genuine reflection of inclusive practice, not favourable intake.
What the school did — 7 years of sustained work
  • ✦ Inclusion at the centre of every decision — not a bolt-on policy, but the starting point for all leadership choices including timetabling, staffing, and curriculum
  • ✦ Calm, orderly, consistent culture built from scratch over 7 years after joining Leigh Academies Trust in 2018 with low family confidence and inconsistent routines
  • ✦ High expectations for all pupils including SEND — never lowering the bar, but matching ambition with structured support
  • ✦ "Small school" model — every student is known well by staff; strong staff–student relationships are structural, not incidental
  • ✦ Proactive attendance systems — robust processes, relentless family communication, and swift supportive intervention achieve above-national attendance including for SEND and disadvantaged pupils
  • ✦ Comprehensive transition and induction programme for mid-year joiners and pupils with additional needs including autism — not a standard "first day" approach
  • ✦ Trust-level support: subject specialist curriculum advisers, leadership development forums, central services that free teachers to focus on teaching, inclusion, and outcomes
Ofsted's exact words on inclusion and SEND

"Leaders are exceedingly successful in removing barriers to attendance."
"The school's success in developing all pupils' character is remarkable."
What this means for your school
The Halley case study demonstrates that exceptional SEND and inclusion outcomes are achievable in high-need, diverse, urban comprehensive schools — but require sustained, multi-year commitment at leadership level. There are no short-cuts: the 7-year transformation timeline is itself the finding. The strategies in this resource — consistent routines, high expectations, strong relationships, adaptive teaching, and evidence-aligned TA deployment — are all visible in what Halley describes. The variable is whether they are implemented consistently, school-wide, over time.
